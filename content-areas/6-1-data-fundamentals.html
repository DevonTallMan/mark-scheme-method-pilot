6.1.1 — The Differences and Links Between Data, Information and Knowledge

The DIKW model is one of the most consistently examined topics in Content Area 6. Know the definitions precisely and practise the Impact component.

SPEC: 6.1.1

WHAT IS DATA?

Data is raw facts and figures before they have been processed. On its own, data has no meaning. The string NAT23%7 is data — without context, it is meaningless.

Data can be qualitative (non-numerical: colours, names, opinions) or quantitative (numerical: temperatures, prices, counts). It can consist of letters, numbers, symbols, graphics, and sound.

KEY TERMS — 6.1.1
DataRaw facts and figures that have no meaning until they are processed.
Qualitative dataNon-numerical data — for example a customer review or a product colour.
Quantitative dataNumerical data — for example a sale price or a stock level.
InformationData that has been given structure, context and meaning.
KnowledgeThe ability to use information to understand something, form judgements, make predictions and take decisions.
WisdomThe ability to apply knowledge to make sound, long-term decisions.

FROM DATA TO INFORMATION

Information is created when data is given structure, context and meaning. The formula is:

Information = Data + Structure + Context + Meaning

Example: 15, cabbages, rabbits is data with no meaning. In context — "15 cabbage plants were eaten by the rabbits" — it becomes information because you now understand what happened.

Structure also matters. The date 24/04/2017 follows the structure DD/MM/YYYY. Without knowing that structure, you cannot use the data reliably.

// EXAMINER TIP

The exam frequently asks for the difference between data and information. The key distinction is context — data has none; information does. Do not just write "information has been processed" — explain what processing adds: structure, context and meaning. That earns the Explain mark.

THE DIKW PYRAMID

The relationship between data, information, knowledge and wisdom is represented as a pyramid. Each layer depends on the layer below it. As you move up, the level of understanding and usefulness increases.

LEVELDEFINITIONEXAMPLE (WEATHER SCENARIO)
DataRaw facts and figures with no meaning.17, 23, 08, cold
InformationData processed with structure, context and meaning.The temperature was 8°C at 5pm on 17th March, rising to 23°C by midday.
KnowledgeInformation stored and applied to form judgements and make predictions.Temperatures rise sharply by midday in spring — outdoor events should avoid the early morning.
WisdomThe ability to apply knowledge to make sound, long-term decisions.The business schedules all outdoor events between 11am and 3pm from March onwards.

// EXAMINER ANNOTATION

6.1.2 — Why Organisations Need Data and Information and How They Use It

The decision-making tier table (strategic / tactical / operational) is one of the highest-frequency exam topics. The Impact mark goes to students who name the consequence — not just the tier.

SPEC: 6.1.2

DATA AS AN ORGANISATIONAL ASSET

Data is described as an organisation's most valuable asset. All organisations need data and information to make correct decisions. Without it, an organisation cannot monitor performance, understand its customers, or plan for the future.

Organisations use data and information to:

  • Analyse market trends to identify patterns that inform decisions
  • Analyse system performance to maintain digital infrastructure
  • Monitor users to improve the experience of their digital systems
  • Use targeted marketing to reach specific customer groups
  • Inform strategic, tactical and operational decision making
  • Carry out threat and opportunity assessment — break-even analysis, predictive models, cost analysis, market trends

STRATEGIC, TACTICAL AND OPERATIONAL DECISIONS

Organisations use data to make three types of decision. These are linked and depend on each other — operational decisions feed into tactical ones, which in turn inform strategic ones.

DECISION TYPEDESCRIPTIONLEVELEXAMPLE
StrategicLong-term, complex decisions that shape the direction of the organisation.Senior managers / directorsNegotiating new supplier contracts based on long-term cost analysis and market trend data.
TacticalMedium-term decisions that implement the strategy. Made within departments.Middle managers / heads of departmentIncreasing or decreasing the number of employees in a department based on performance reports.
OperationalShort-term, routine, day-to-day decisions. Simple and repetitive.Junior managers / supervisorsCreating staff rotas for the week based on bookings data and shift availability.
// EXAMINER TIP — DECISION TIERS

This is the highest-frequency topic in 6.1. Students lose marks by only identifying the decision type and the level. The Impact mark requires you to explain what happens to the organisation if the wrong level makes the decision — for example, a junior supervisor making a strategic decision without the authority or long-term data needed could result in costly contractual commitments or supply chain failures.

MARKET TRENDS

Market analysis is the assessment of quantitative and qualitative characteristics of a specific market. Organisations spot trends and respond before competitors do. Trends can change rapidly, particularly those driven by social media.

TARGETED MARKETING

Rather than broadcasting to everyone, targeted marketing uses purchase history, browsing behaviour, and demographic data to send relevant promotions to specific customer segments. More cost-effective than mass marketing.

SYSTEM PERFORMANCE

Organisations monitor CPU utilisation, cache miss rates, response times and user request completion rates using a dashboard — a graphical interface presenting metrics in real time. Trends allow engineers to anticipate failures.

MONITORING USERS

Website traffic data reveals how long users spend on a page, how many clicks they take to complete a task, and where they abandon a process. This data drives improvements to the user experience and recovers lost sales.

KEY TERMS — 6.1.2
MetricsA set of numbers that gives information about a particular process or activity.
DashboardA type of graphical user interface providing simple visualisation of data related to performance indicators, capable of showing real-time data updates.

EXAM QUESTION [4 MARKS]

A junior supervisor at a sports retailer decides to terminate the contract with the company's main stock supplier. Identify the type of decision this is, explain why it is inappropriate for a supervisor to make this decision, and explain one impact on the organisation.

✗ WEAK ANSWER

// EXAMINER ANNOTATION
N: correct. E: weak — "not their job" does not explain what a strategic decision involves or why it requires senior authority and data access. I: too vague — "problems for the business" names no specific consequence. 2/4 marks.
✓ FULL-MARK ANSWER
This is a strategic decision [N]. Strategic decisions are long-term and complex — terminating a supplier contract has financial and operational implications that require analysis of cost data, market alternatives and long-term supply chain planning, which only senior management have the authority and information to carry out [E]. If a junior supervisor makes this decision without the required data and authority, the organisation could find itself without a reliable supply of stock, leading to product shortages, loss of sales and reputational damage with customers [I].

6.1.3 — How Data Is Generated

The ML life cycle is a near-certain exam topic. The IoT and transactional data sections require applied N·E·I answers — not just definitions.

SPEC: 6.1.3

SOURCES OF DATA

SOURCEDESCRIPTION
Human generatedData created by people using digital systems — emails, social media posts, form submissions, documents. The Conversation (2021) estimated 500 million tweets and 294 billion emails are sent every day.
AI / Machine LearningData produced or labelled by AI systems as they learn from and process information.
SensorsPhysical devices that measure environmental or physical properties and convert them to digital signals.
Internet of Things (IoT)Networks of connected smart devices that collect and transmit data automatically.
Transactional dataData created when a transaction occurs between an organisation and a customer or another organisation.

AI VS ML — THE DISTINCTION THAT LOSES MARKS

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the science of training machines to perform tasks that would normally require human intelligence — speech recognition, image identification, decision making.

Machine Learning (ML) is a subset of AI. Rather than being programmed with explicit rules, an ML system learns patterns from large amounts of data and uses those patterns to draw conclusions — just as a person reasons from experience.

// EXAMINER TIP — AI vs ML

Students frequently conflate AI and ML or treat them as identical. In an exam, always state that ML is a subset of AI and then describe what makes ML distinct: it learns from data rather than following fixed, pre-programmed rules. Both points are needed for full marks on a distinction question.

THE MACHINE LEARNING LIFE CYCLE

ML systems are built through a structured, iterative process. Errors at any stage affect the reliability of the final model's outputs — this is the most important thing to understand for the exam.

STAGENAMEWHAT HAPPENS / WHY IT MATTERS
1Gathering dataRelevant data is collected from sources such as sensors, user inputs and databases. Quality and relevance here determines everything — irrelevant data wastes processing and distorts outputs.
2Data preparationRaw data is cleaned and formatted into a consistent structure. Missing values, errors and inconsistent formats are corrected here.
3Data wranglingData is transformed and organised to meet the specific requirements of the analysis. May involve combining datasets, recategorising values, or removing outliers that could skew results.
4Analyse dataPatterns, correlations and insights are identified. Statistical techniques and algorithms are applied to the prepared data.
5Train modelThe ML algorithm is fed the prepared data and begins learning patterns. The cleaner and more relevant the data, the more accurately the model learns.
6Test modelThe trained model is tested against a separate dataset it has not seen before. This reveals whether it has learned real patterns or just memorised the training data.
7DeploymentThe validated model is released for use with real incoming data to make predictions or classifications.
N·E·I WORKED EXAMPLE 3 — ML LIFE CYCLE
EXAM QUESTION [4 MARKS]

Explain the role of the data wrangling stage in the machine learning life cycle and explain one impact on the organisation if this stage is skipped before the model is deployed.

✗ WEAK ANSWER
Data wrangling is the third stage of the ML life cycle. It involves organising and transforming data. If it is skipped, the model might not work properly and could give wrong answers.
// EXAMINER ANNOTATION
N: correct. E: vague — "organising and transforming" is present but the answer does not explain why transformation is necessary or what it involves. I: "wrong answers" is too vague — no specific organisational consequence is named. 2/4 marks.
✓ FULL-MARK ANSWER
Data wrangling is the stage in the ML life cycle where raw, unstructured data is transformed and reorganised to meet the specific requirements of the model being trained [N]. This involves combining datasets from different sources, removing outliers that could distort results, recategorising values for consistency, and structuring the data so the algorithm can process it effectively [E]. If this stage is skipped, the model will be trained on disorganised, inconsistent data, meaning it learns incorrect or irrelevant patterns — when deployed, it will generate unreliable predictions, for example a stock recommendation system could suggest incorrect reorder quantities, leading to product shortages or overstock and significant financial loss for the organisation [I].

IoT — THE INTERNET OF THINGS

The Internet of Things is a network of smart physical devices connected to each other and to the internet, exchanging data without requiring direct human intervention.

All smart IoT devices have four core components:

  • Sensors — to collect data from the physical environment
  • Connectivity — to transmit data to other devices or the internet
  • Data processing — to analyse the data collected
  • A user interface (UI) — to present information or allow control
KEY TERMS — 6.1.3
AIThe science of training machines to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence, such as speech recognition, visual identification and decision making.
Machine Learning (ML)A subset of AI in which a system learns patterns from data rather than following explicitly programmed rules, enabling it to make predictions and draw conclusions.
IoTA collection of smart devices that connect to each other and exchange data using the internet.
D2D / M2MDevice-to-device or machine-to-machine communication — data transferred directly between devices without human involvement.
Smart CityAn urban area that uses IoT and digital devices to collect data, enabling well-informed decisions that improve quality of life for those who live and work there.
Transactional dataData about transactions that occur between an organisation and its customers, or between organisations.
